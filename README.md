Accountability Layer Protocol (ALP)
A Human-Anchored AI Governance Framework
Principal Architect: Jesse Kransky | v1.7.0 | May 2026

What It Is
The Accountability Layer Protocol (ALP) is a governance framework designed to close the Accountability Gap in modern AI deployment — the systemic fault line where organizations treat synthetic output as a weightless tool, bypassing the human accountability structures that absorb legal, financial, and institutional risk.

The ALP does not restrict what AI can do. It ensures that every high-stakes synthetic output is authorized, logged, and traceable to a human being who accepted responsibility for it.

The Problem
AI systems are being deployed into fiscal forecasting, workforce management, critical infrastructure, and legal decision-making without load-bearing accountability structures. When something goes wrong — and it will — there is no forensic trail, no human signature, and no clear line of liability.

This is not an engineering problem. It is a governance problem. And it is not theoretical. It is happening now.

The Framework
The ALP addresses the Accountability Gap through four structural mechanisms:

1. Semantic Validation — The system requires a signed human statement of intent before executing any high-stakes task. The AI cannot proceed on assumption.

2. The Liability Forecaster — Before execution, the system runs a recursive risk simulation and locks until the human manually accepts the projected probability of failure.

3. The Bio-Anchor — Authorization requires a physical cryptographic key tied to the end user's verified hardware. Remote rubber-stamping is structurally impossible.

4. Elastic Cognitive Proof-of-Work — For the highest-stakes decisions, execution locks until the human solves a unique logic challenge. This prevents reflex-signing during decision fatigue.

Human override is permitted — but it requires a signed digital waiver accepting 100% personal liability, written to an immutable audit ledger that cannot be altered or deleted.
